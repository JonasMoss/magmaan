\section*{Purpose}

This note records what is needed to compute the full regular eigenspectrum for
nested tests in the profile-Hessian paradigm, and a first finite-sample smoke
test in a continuous GLS setting. The companion scripts are
\texttt{relative-and-nested-fit-check.py}, \texttt{gls\_nested\_profile\_mc.py},
and \texttt{gls\_nested\_cfa\_cycle\_scan.py}.
The finite-sample script uses the C++ helper
\texttt{weighted\_chisq\_quantile\_cli.cpp}, which calls \texttt{magmaan}'s
\texttt{robust::weighted\_chisq\_quantile} and
\texttt{robust::weighted\_chisq\_upper} for deterministic mixture quantiles. The
only Monte Carlo in the smoke is the finite-sample data generation.

\section{Required Objects}

Let $x$ be the first-stage vector and let
\[
  \sqrt n(\hat x-x_0)\Rightarrow Z,\qquad Z\sim N(0,\Gamma_x).
\]
For each model $M$, define the profiled value
\[
  v_M(x)=\min_\theta f_M(\theta,x).
\]
The nested contrast is
\[
  c(x)=v_B(x)-v_A(x),
\]
where $B$ is the restricted model and $A$ is the larger model. Under the regular
nested pseudo-null,
\[
  c(x_0)=0,\qquad \nabla c(x_0)=0.
\]
The reference law is therefore
\[
  n\,c(\hat x)\Rightarrow \frac12 Z'(Q_B-Q_A)Z
\]
if $f$ is scaled as a half-discrepancy, or equivalently
\[
  2n\,c(\hat x)\Rightarrow Z'(Q_B-Q_A)Z.
\]
Thus the full spectrum is obtained from the self-adjoint version of
\[
  (Q_B-Q_A)\Gamma_x .
\]
In implementation terms, the steps are:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Build a common first-stage vector $x$ and covariance $\Gamma_x$.
\item Compute the profiled score $s_M(x)=\nabla_x v_M(x)$ for each model.
\item Differentiate it to get $Q_M=D_xs_M(x_0)$.
\item Verify the pseudo-null conditions $v_B=v_A$ and $s_B=s_A$.
\item Eigensolve $\Gamma_x^{1/2}(Q_B-Q_A)\Gamma_x^{1/2}$.
\end{enumerate}
The old $U_B-U_A$ spectrum is the special exact-fit/fixed-weight/Gauss--Newton
case. It is not the general object.

\section{GLS Smoke Design}

The smoke uses the linear covariance example from
\texttt{relative-and-nested-fit.tex}. There are four observed variables.
\[
  B:\ \text{compound symmetry: one variance and one covariance},
\]
\[
  A:\ \text{one common variance and free covariances}.
\]
The test is equality of the covariances. The normal-theory GLS weight is
estimated from the first-stage covariance:
\[
  W(u)=\Gamma_{\mathrm{NT}}(u)^{-1}.
\]

To make the nested pseudo-null exact while keeping both models misspecified, the
population covariance is diagonal with unequal variances:
\[
  \Sigma_0=I+\epsilon\operatorname{diag}(d),
  \qquad
  d=(1,-1,1,-1).
\]
Both models share the same pseudo-true zero covariance structure, so
\[
  v_B(x_0)-v_A(x_0)=0,\qquad s_B(x_0)-s_A(x_0)=0,
\]
but the common-variance part is misspecified when $\epsilon\ne0$.

\section{Population Spectrum}

For $\epsilon=0.15$, the population script reports:
\[
  v_B-v_A=0,\qquad \lVert s_B-s_A\rVert=0,
\]
and the profile/value finite-difference Hessians agree to $1.17\times10^{-7}$.
The classical fixed-weight spectrum is
\[
  (1,1,1,1,1),
\]
whereas the profile-Hessian spectrum is
\[
  (1.4082,\ 0.9139,\ 0.9139,\ 0.9139,\ 0.5057),
  \qquad
  \tr=4.6557 .
\]
So even in this linear model with no model-curvature term, estimating the GLS
weight changes the reference law under fixed misspecification.

\section{A High-Contrast Stress Test}

The population scan in \texttt{gls\_nested\_profile\_scan.py} searches diagonal
pseudo-null misspecifications before running Monte Carlo. The useful heuristic is
simple: the fixed-weight $U$ spectrum stays close to the exact-fit reference,
whereas the profile spectrum changes through the derivative of the estimated GLS
weight. Those derivative terms are driven by the pseudo-true residual, so large
variance heterogeneity is the first place to look.

One strong $p=4$ stress case uses a linear diagonal pattern with
\(\epsilon=0.85\), so the marginal variances range from \(0.15\) to \(1.85\).
The data-generating law is
\[
  Y\sim N_4(0,\Sigma_0),\qquad
  \Sigma_0=
  \begin{pmatrix}
  0.1500 & 0 & 0 & 0\\
  0 & 0.7167 & 0 & 0\\
  0 & 0 & 1.2833 & 0\\
  0 & 0 & 0 & 1.8500
  \end{pmatrix}.
\]
The larger model \(A\) is common variance with free covariances,
\[
  \Sigma_A(a,c)=
  \begin{pmatrix}
  a & c_{12} & c_{13} & c_{14}\\
  c_{12} & a & c_{23} & c_{24}\\
  c_{13} & c_{23} & a & c_{34}\\
  c_{14} & c_{24} & c_{34} & a
  \end{pmatrix},
\]
and the restricted model \(B\) is compound symmetry,
\[
  \Sigma_B(a,b)=
  \begin{pmatrix}
  a & b & b & b\\
  b & a & b & b\\
  b & b & a & b\\
  b & b & b & a
  \end{pmatrix}.
\]
At the population point the normal-theory GLS weight is
\(W_0=\Gamma_{\mathrm{NT}}(\Sigma_0)^{-1}\). Because \(\Sigma_0\) is diagonal,
the off-diagonal moment weights are diagonal and all true covariances are zero,
so both models choose zero covariance:
\[
  c_{ij}^{*}=0,\qquad b^{*}=0.
\]
The common variance is the GLS projection of the four true variances onto one
number,
\[
  a^{*}=
  \frac{\sum_i w_i\sigma_i^2}{\sum_i w_i},\qquad
  w_i=\frac{1}{2\sigma_i^4},
\]
which gives
\[
  a_A^{*}=a_B^{*}=0.1983847695.
\]
Thus
\[
  \Sigma_A^{*}=\Sigma_B^{*}=0.1983847695\,I_4,
\]
while both models are badly misspecified as approximations to \(\Sigma_0\).
The population half-discrepancies are equal,
\[
  f_A(\Sigma_0)=f_B(\Sigma_0)=0.5346995918,
\]
so this is a genuine nested pseudo-null: the less restricted and more
restricted models share the same pseudo-true covariance even though neither
model is true.

The profile spectrum is
\[
  (0.5401,\ 0.3781,\ 0.3279,\ 0.2767,\ 0.1990),
\]
giving a profile 0.95 cutoff of \(3.9305\), versus the ordinary
\(\chi^2_5\) cutoff \(11.0705\). The finite-sample smoke again used \(12000\)
replications:
\[
\begin{array}{c|ccc}
N & 150 & 600 & 3000\\
\hline
\Pr(T>q_{0.95}^{\mathrm{profile}}) & 0.0404 & 0.0475 & 0.0508\\
\Pr(T>q_{0.95}^{\chi^2_5}) & 0.0000 & 0.0001 & 0.0000
\end{array}
\]
So the ordinary chi-square reference is not mildly conservative in this stress
case; it is effectively unusable for a nominal 0.05 test.

\subsection*{A round three-indicator conservative example}

The phenomenon is not tied to four indicators, and it does not require nonround
decimals. For \(p=3\), take the diagonal
pseudo-null
\[
  \Sigma_0=\operatorname{diag}(4,\ 4,\ 1).
\]
The same model pair is used: model \(A\) has one common variance and three free
covariances, while model \(B\) has one common variance and one common
covariance. The population GLS projections again coincide,
\[
  \Sigma_A^{*}=\Sigma_B^{*}=\frac{4}{3}I_3,
  \qquad f_A(\Sigma_0)=f_B(\Sigma_0)=0.25.
\]
The profile spectrum has only two positive weights,
\[
  (0.4444,\ 0.1481),
\]
so the profile 0.95 cutoff is \(1.9034\), while the ordinary \(\chi^2_2\)
cutoff is \(5.9915\). Under the profile law, the ordinary cutoff has tail
probability \(0.00030\). In \(12000\) replications, the profile cutoff rejected at
\[
  0.0478,\quad 0.0480,\quad 0.0508
\]
for \(N=150,600,3000\), while the ordinary chi-square cutoff rejected at
\[
  0.0007,\quad 0.0003,\quad 0.0003.
\]

\subsection*{A round one-degree liberal example}

The same mechanism can also move in the opposite direction; the conservative
examples above are not a theorem about GLS. Keep \(p=3\), but use the simpler
nested contrast that tests one covariance:
\[
  B:\ \Sigma_B(a)=aI_3,\qquad
  A:\ \Sigma_A(a,c)=
  \begin{pmatrix}
  a & c & 0\\
  c & a & 0\\
  0 & 0 & a
  \end{pmatrix}.
\]
Now take
\[
  \Sigma_0=\operatorname{diag}(4,\ 4,\ 7).
\]
The true covariance between the first two variables is zero, and the first two
variances are equal. The extra covariance parameter in \(A\) therefore remains
zero at the population optimum even though both models are misspecified by the
third variance. The GLS projection is
\[
  \Sigma_A^{*}=\Sigma_B^{*}=\frac{84}{19}I_3,
  \qquad f_A(\Sigma_0)=f_B(\Sigma_0)=0.0394736842.
\]
This is again a genuine nested pseudo-null: the value and profile gradient
differences are zero at \(\Sigma_0\). The profile spectrum is
\[
  (1.4654),
\]
so the profile 0.95 cutoff is \(5.6292\), while the ordinary \(\chi^2_1\)
cutoff is \(3.8415\). Under the profile law, the ordinary cutoff has tail
probability \(0.1054\), so the nominal 0.05 test is asymptotically about a
10.5 percent test. In \(12000\) replications, the profile cutoff rejected at
\[
  0.0466,\quad 0.0453,\quad 0.0478
\]
for \(N=150,600,3000\), while the ordinary chi-square cutoff rejected at
\[
  0.1009,\quad 0.1048,\quad 0.1031.
\]

\subsection*{Population fit-index scale}

For orientation, the following table reports population RMSEA and SRMR for the
misspecified models used above. Here \(F_0=2f_M(\Sigma_0)\) is the full
population GLS discrepancy, so
\[
  \varepsilon_{0,M}=\sqrt{F_0/df_M}.
\]
The degrees of freedom \(df_A\) and \(df_B\) are the ordinary model degrees of
freedom against the saturated covariance model, not the nested-test df. The
reported SRMR is the covariance-only population value
\[
  \operatorname{SRMR}_0 =
  \left\{
  \frac{2}{p(p+1)}
  \sum_{i\le j}
  \left(
  \frac{\Sigma_{0,ij}-\Sigma^*_{ij}}
       {\sqrt{\Sigma_{0,ii}\Sigma_{0,jj}}}
  \right)^2
  \right\}^{1/2}.
\]
Because \(A\) and \(B\) share the same pseudo-true covariance in these
pseudo-null examples, they also share the same \(F_0\) and SRMR. RMSEA differs
only because it divides by the model's own overall df.

\subsection*{Growth with the number of indicators}

The conservative failure is not a finite-\(p\) artifact. A clean construction is
block replication. Take \(K\) independent copies of the round conservative
three-indicator pseudo-null block above, and test the same within-block
covariance equality restriction in each block. This is a block-diagonal SEM
sequence rather than the single global compound-symmetry family, but it is
enough to show that the issue does not disappear with more indicators. The
ordinary reference has \(2K\) degrees of freedom. The profile reference is
\[
  \sum_{k=1}^{K}\{0.4444\,\chi^2_{1k,1}+0.1481\,\chi^2_{1k,2}\}.
\]
Its mean is \(K(0.4444+0.1481)=0.5926K\), whereas the ordinary
\(\chi^2_{2K}\) reference has mean \(2K\). Therefore the ordinary 0.95 cutoff,
which is \(2K+O(\sqrt K)\), lies far to the right of the true profile mixture
mean by an order-\(K\) amount. By concentration,
\[
  \Pr_{\mathrm{profile}}\{T>q_{0.95}(\chi^2_{2K})\}\to 0.
\]
So the nominal 0.05 chi-square test can be made arbitrarily conservative, in
the sense that its true size tends to zero as the number of replicated
indicator blocks grows. The absolute size error is then as large as possible
for a conservative 0.05-level test.

This is intentionally an extreme design. The practical next question is how
large the gap remains under less ill-conditioned but still realistic covariance
patterns, and then under ordinal DWLS where the first-stage weight inputs are
more variable.

\section{A More SEM-Like Pseudo-Null}

The diagonal examples isolate the mechanism, but they make the population too
simple. A more SEM-like construction is a one-factor population with omitted
local dependence:
\[
  \Sigma_0(\delta)
  = \lambda^2 \mathbf 1\mathbf 1'
    + (1-\lambda^2)I_6
    + \delta C_6,
  \qquad \lambda=0.7,
\]
where \(C_6\) is the adjacency matrix of the six-cycle. Thus all population
off-diagonal correlations are positive: non-adjacent pairs have correlation
\(0.49\), while adjacent local-dependence pairs have correlation
\(0.49+\delta\).

The nested pair is now nonlinear:
\[
  A:\ \Sigma_A(\lambda_i,\theta_i)=\lambda\lambda' + \operatorname{diag}(\theta_i),
  \qquad
  B:\ \Sigma_B(\ell,\theta_i)=\ell^2\mathbf 1\mathbf 1'
    + \operatorname{diag}(\theta_i).
\]
Model \(A\) is congeneric one-factor CFA and \(B\) is tau-equivalent CFA. The
six-cycle symmetry is transitive over indicators, so the pseudo-true congeneric
fit has equal loadings and equal residual variances. Hence the larger-model
pseudo-true point lies in \(B\), even though both models are misspecified for
\(\delta>0\). Numerically, \(\lvert f_B-f_A\rvert<1.4\times10^{-17}\) and
\(\lVert s_B-s_A\rVert<4.2\times10^{-11}\) over the grid below.

The ordinary nested reference is \(\chi^2_5\), whose 0.95 cutoff is \(11.0705\).
The profile-Hessian reference has six positive weights in this family; under
estimated weights and misspecification, the number of positive directions need
not equal the parameter-count difference. The population scan gives:
\[
\begin{array}{c|c|ccc|cc}
\delta & \text{corr. range} & \text{RMSEA}_A & \text{RMSEA}_B & \text{SRMR}
& q^{\mathrm{profile}}_{0.95}
& \Pr_{\mathrm{profile}}\{T>q_{0.95}(\chi^2_5)\}\\
\hline
0.02 & [0.49,0.51] & 0.025 & 0.020 & 0.008 & 11.053 & 0.050\\
0.04 & [0.49,0.53] & 0.051 & 0.041 & 0.017 & 10.997 & 0.049\\
0.06 & [0.49,0.55] & 0.077 & 0.062 & 0.027 & 10.896 & 0.047\\
0.08 & [0.49,0.57] & 0.104 & 0.084 & 0.037 & 10.740 & 0.044\\
0.10 & [0.49,0.59] & 0.133 & 0.107 & 0.050 & 10.514 & 0.040\\
0.12 & [0.49,0.61] & 0.164 & 0.131 & 0.064 & 10.196 & 0.035
\end{array}
\]

This is a useful contrast to the toy stress tests. With acceptable global fit
the ordinary chi-square reference is not catastrophically wrong in this family;
the asymptotic 0.05 tail is around \(0.047\)--\(0.049\) for RMSEA near
\(0.05\)--\(0.08\) and SRMR below \(0.03\). The distortion becomes visible as
local dependence grows: at SRMR \(0.037\), the ordinary test is about a 0.044
test, and at SRMR \(0.050\), about a 0.040 test. This suggests a practical
performance study rather than another extreme example: grid the local-dependence
strength, loading level, number of indicators, and \(N\), and report empirical
tail error alongside population RMSEA/SRMR and the profile spectrum.

\section{Magmaan Machinery}

The reusable library pieces are now:
\[
  \texttt{robust::compute\_profile\_contrast\_spectrum}(Q,\Gamma),
\]
which returns the positive eigenvalues of
$\Gamma^{1/2}Q\Gamma^{1/2}$ and flags genuinely negative directions, and
\[
  \texttt{robust::weighted\_chisq\_upper},\qquad
  \texttt{robust::weighted\_chisq\_quantile},
\]
which provide the central positive weighted-\(\chi^2\) reference law and its
cutoffs. The old \texttt{robust::imhof\_upper} symbol remains as a compatibility
alias, but the new call sites use the distribution-level name.

\subsection*{Spectrum-size bookkeeping}

The new profile-Hessian methods should report the inertia of the reference law,
not only the ordinary model or nested-test degrees of freedom. Let \(Q\) denote
the symmetric profile Hessian in the first-stage moment coordinates and let
\(\Gamma\) denote the first-stage covariance. The computable spectrum is that of
\(\Gamma^{1/2}Q\Gamma^{1/2}\), equivalently the nonzero spectrum of
\(Q\Gamma\). If \(\Gamma\) is positive definite and \(Q\) is held fixed,
Sylvester's law gives the same positive, negative, and zero counts as \(Q\).
Thus \(\Gamma\) changes the magnitudes but not the signs. If \(\Gamma\) is
singular, this statement holds only after restricting to the range of
\(\Gamma\). It is also a conditional statement: in WLS, DWLS, and
estimated-weight constructions, \(Q\) itself may change with the population
moments, the pseudo-true point, and the first-stage weight channel.

Implementation reports should therefore carry at least
\[
  \begin{gathered}
    \#\{\lambda_j>\tau\},\qquad
    \#\{\lambda_j<-\tau\},\qquad
    \#\{|\lambda_j|>\tau\},\qquad
    \operatorname{tr}(Q\Gamma),\\
    \sum_{\lambda_j>\tau}\lambda_j,\qquad
    \sum_{\lambda_j>\tau}\lambda_j^2,
  \end{gathered}
\]
alongside the nominal \(df\). The eigentolerance \(\tau\) must be recorded or
made deterministic from the scale of \(Q\Gamma\). Positive eigenvalues are the
weights for the usual positive weighted-\(\chi^2\) mixture tail. The trace
replacement for bias and RMSEA centering is the signed trace
\(\operatorname{tr}(Q\Gamma)\); it equals the positive-weight sum only in the
PSD or local-null regimes. When negative directions appear, the positive-tail
helper is a diagnostic approximation to an indefinite quadratic form rather
than the full law.

Some of the counting can be done without eigendecomposing the full moment-space
matrix. In the fixed-weight one-metric case,
\[
  Q = V - VD B^{-1}D'V,\qquad A=D'VD,
\]
so the residual complement contributes automatic positive directions and the
remaining sign decisions reduce to the parameter-space pencil
\[
  \nu_j=\operatorname{eig}(B^{-1}A).
\]
Directions with \(\nu_j<1\) add positive curvature, directions with
\(\nu_j>1\) add negative curvature, and \(\nu_j=1\) is the Gauss--Newton
boundary. In the two-metric ML construction the analogous reduced matrix uses
\(A_* = D'W_*V_0^{-1}W_*D\) in place of \(A\). This \(p\)-dimensional spectrum is
a useful gate for whether the profile-curvature correction matters, but it does
not by itself justify falling back to classical RMSEA; non-normal
\(\Gamma\)-based trace corrections may still be needed.

\section{What This Suggests}

For GLS, the full nested eigenspectrum is not hard in principle. The hard part
is not the eigensolve; it is computing $Q_M$ reliably. Finite differences of the
profile score are enough for research scripts. The next production step is to
expose analytic or automatic profile derivatives for:
\[
  s_M(x),\qquad Q_M=D_xs_M(x).
\]

For categorical DWLS, the same steps apply, but $x$ must include the weight
inputs such as $\gamma=\operatorname{diag}(\Gamma_u)$. That makes $Q_M$ a block
Hessian over $(u,\gamma)$ rather than only over ordinal moments. The GLS smoke
is therefore the right first implementation target: it validates the profile
machinery before the ordinal first-stage stack is made larger.
