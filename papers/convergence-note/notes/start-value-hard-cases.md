# Start-Value Hard Cases

This note records the first design reading for the convergence/start-value
paper. The goal is to turn published small-sample nonconvergence setups into
hard-case factories for magmaan.

## Research Opening

The promising question is not whether there is one universally good SEM
starting-value formula. A better framing is:

Can a fast, architecture-aware start-value portfolio improve convergence,
admissibility, and basin selection under a fixed compute budget?

magmaan is well positioned for this because start generation, objective
evaluation, optimizer diagnostics, and corpus-scale fitting are already separate
pieces. The useful comparison is budgeted: for example, what policy wins if it
gets 1, 5, 25, or 150 candidate starts?

## Candidate Policies

- Deterministic starts: simple, FABIN2/FABIN3, Guttman/MGM, Bentler-1982, and
  James-Stein-style CFA starts.
- Bounded random starts: draw candidate variances, covariances, and loadings
  from data-driven ranges, preserving positive definiteness when possible.
- Hybrid starts: deterministic measurement starts plus random latent covariance
  or residual variance starts.
- Screened starts: generate many cheap candidates, score by objective value, PD
  margin, and perhaps one or two safeguarded optimizer steps, then fit only the
  best few.

## De Jonckere and Rosseel 2025

Source PDF:
`resources/Jonckere and Rosseel 2025 - A note on using random starting values in small sample SEM.pdf`.

Their bounded random-start procedure uses the data-driven bounds from
De Jonckere and Rosseel (2022). Regressions are not randomized; they are set to
zero. Latent and residual covariance matrices are generated by drawing
correlations, usually in `[-0.5, 0.5]`, and converting them to covariance
matrices after variance starts are drawn. Loadings are drawn after variances so
their ranges can be scaled by the candidate factor and residual variances. If a
candidate implied covariance is not positive definite, the fit can fail
immediately and another candidate can be drawn.

Study 1 is a two-factor latent regression:

- Model: `X -> Y`.
- Indicators: three per latent.
- Population: marker loadings `lambda1 = lambda4 = 1`; other loadings
  `lambda2 = 0.8`, `lambda3 = 0.6`, `lambda5 = 0.7`, `lambda6 = 0.9`;
  latent/residual variances reported as `1.0`; `beta = 0.1`.
- Data: 1000 datasets, `N = 20`.
- Filter: keep cases where lavaan and OpenMx agree on convergence or failure.
- Convergence definition: optimizer success and standard errors computable.
- Result: roughly 366 default failures, with 318 rescued within 150 bounded
  random-start attempts. Many rescued fits are improper: about 87% have at least
  one negative variance.

Study 2 is a crossloading chain SEM:

- Model: `eta1 -> eta2 -> eta3`.
- Indicators: three per factor.
- Main loadings: `1.0`.
- Crossloadings: `lambda4`, `lambda8`, and `lambda9` are `0.3`.
- Structural regressions: `beta1 = 0.1`, `beta2 = 0.2`.
- Latent residual variances: `1.0`.
- Indicator residual variances: `0.51` for `y1,y2,y3,y5,y8,y9`; `0.2895` for
  `y4,y6,y7`.
- Data: 1000 datasets, `N = 20`.
- Result: 341 default failures. About 25% are rescued by one random start, and
  only about 4% remain nonconverged after 150 random-start attempts. About 90%
  of rescued fits have at least one negative variance.

Study 3 is a multistate-single-trait model:

- Model: second-order CFA / single-level MSST over three time points.
- Reported state loadings: `1, 0.5, 1.3`.
- Reported trait loadings: `1, 0.5, 1.3`.
- Reported measurement errors: `0.6, 0.25, 0.7`.
- Data: 1000 datasets, `N = 10`.
- Result: 385 default failures, 221 rescued within 150 random-start attempts.
  All rescued cases have at least one negative variance.
- Caveat: the paper says the remaining true values live in the OSF repository,
  so the current magmaan helper uses explicit defaults until we mirror the OSF
  script.

For magmaan, the most useful cases are likely not the default failures alone.
The richest targets are the cases that stay failed after many starts, the cases
that converge only to improper solutions, and cases where different starts reach
distinct local optima.

## De Jonckere and Rosseel 2022

Source PDF:
`resources/De Jonckere and Rosseel 2022 - Using bounded estimation to avoid nonconvergence in small sample structural equation modeling.pdf`.

This paper supplies the bounds later reused for random starting values. It is
mostly about bounded estimation rather than start generation.

Study 1 uses the same two-factor latent regression family:

- Population: `X -> Y`, three indicators per latent, loadings `1, 0.8, 0.6` on
  both factors, all residual variances `1.0`, `beta = 0.25`.
- Sample sizes: `10,15,20,25,30,40,50,60,70,80,90,100`.
- Replications: 10,000 per sample size.
- Methods: no bounds, zero lower bounds on observed residual variances,
  standard bounds, wide bounds.
- No-bounds failures are large: 5115/10000 at `N = 10`, 2973/10000 at `N = 20`,
  and 522/10000 at `N = 50`.
- Wide bounds essentially remove failures for this design.

Study 2 uses the crossloading chain SEM:

- Population: `beta1 = beta2 = 0.6`.
- Main loadings: `1.0`.
- Crossloadings: `0.3`.
- Latent residual variances: `1.0`.
- Indicator residual variances: same `0.51` / `0.2895` pattern as the 2025
  paper.
- Fitted models: M0 correct; M1 omits one crossloading; M2 omits two; M3 omits
  three.
- Sample sizes shown: `N = 10` to `30`, 10,000 replications per cell.
- No-bounds M0 at `N = 10` fails 6850/10000 times; standard and wide bounds
  reduce this to 50 and 40 failures.

Important caveat: in the 2022 crossloading study, the population values were
used as starting values. That makes the design useful for boundary and
small-sample hardness, but not a pure start-value stress test.

## Ludtke, Ulitzsch, and Robitzsch 2021

Source PDF:
`resources/Lüdtke et al. 2021 - A comparison of penalized maximum likelihood estima ... stimating confirmatory factor analysis models with small sample sizes.pdf`.

Their first simulation is a clean CFA admissibility stress test:

- Model: two-factor CFA, three indicators per factor.
- Standardized loadings: `0.50`.
- Observed variances: `1`.
- Single-indicator reliability: `0.25`; scale reliability roughly `0.50`.
- Latent correlations: `0.30`, `0.50`, `0.70`.
- Sample sizes: `30`, `50`, `100`.
- Replications: 1000 per cell.
- Unconstrained ML has frequent estimation problems. At `N = 30`, convergence
  plus admissible latent correlation is only about 51%, 57%, and 53% for the
  three correlations.
- Constrained ML converges in all replications but often lands on boundaries.

Their second simulation keeps the same two-factor CFA but generates nonnormal
observed variables with prescribed skewness/kurtosis. This is worth adding
later, but the first root R helper implements the normal design first.

## Hardness Filters for magmaan

Because NLopt L-BFGS rarely fails, the paper should not define hardness only as
optimizer nonconvergence. Better filters:

- optimizer failure or non-finite objective;
- final gradient norm above tolerance;
- implied covariance close to singular or not positive definite along the path;
- negative observed or latent variances;
- latent correlations outside or near the admissible range;
- standard errors unavailable or information matrix singular;
- large objective gap relative to a heavy multistart reference;
- distinct local basins across starts;
- severe disagreement between converged solutions and known population values.

That gives us a richer study even if magmaan converges where lavaan struggles.
